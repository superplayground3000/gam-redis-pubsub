## 1. Failover latency — random kill (operator-observed distribution)

Active leader force-deleted (`--grace-period=0 --force`) at an arbitrary point in the renew cycle; failover = `acquireTime(new) − t_kill`. Source + sink pooled.

| config | duration/renew/retry | n | min | p50 | p95 | max | mean | soft ceiling* |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| tight | 3s/2s/1s | 20 | 3.11 | 3.58 | 4.57 | 5.10 | 3.75 | ~5.2s |
| default | 6s/4s/1s | 20 | 5.79 | 6.92 | 7.63 | 7.83 | 6.88 | ~8.2s |
| stock | 15s/10s/2s | 20 | 15.05 | 16.69 | 18.58 | 19.50 ⚠️ | 16.92 | ~19.4s |

*soft ceiling = `LeaseDuration + RetryPeriod·(1+JitterFactor)`, `JitterFactor=1.2` — the longest a standby's *jittered* acquire-retry can take after lease expiry. It is an **approximate** bound: it omits the API round-trip and the kill's intra-renew-cycle phase, so a small tail can exceed it. Here the stock random max (19.50s, +0.1s) and the default post-renew max (§4, 8.36s, +0.16s) sit just over — the p95s are comfortably under. Treat it as 'where the bulk lands,' not a hard guarantee. Source and sink legs are pooled because their p50s match closely (tight 3.54/3.85s, default 6.92/6.94s, stock 17.01/16.41s source/sink).

## 2. Decomposition — LeaseDuration vs retry/jitter

`failover = lease_remaining + acquire_overhead`, where `lease_remaining = (renewTime_old + LeaseDuration) − t_kill` is the time the standby must wait for the dead leader's lease to expire, and `acquire_overhead` is the post-expiry retry+jitter+API cost. Means over the random-kill trials:

| config | mean lease_remaining | mean acquire_overhead | mean failover |
|---|---|---|---|
| tight | 2.72s | 1.02s | 3.75s |
| default | 5.73s | 1.15s | 6.88s |
| stock | 14.77s | 2.15s | 16.92s |

The overhead term is governed by `RetryPeriod` (and jitter), not `LeaseDuration`: it is ~1.0–1.2s for tight and default (both `RetryPeriod=1s`) and ~2.2s for stock (`RetryPeriod=2s`) — i.e. it scales with `RetryPeriod`, not constant in absolute terms. Either way it is small and roughly fixed *within a given `RetryPeriod`*, so the large failover spread between configs comes almost entirely from `lease_remaining`, i.e. from `LeaseDuration` (2.7s → 5.7s → 14.8s).

## 3. Clock-domain corroboration (acquire vs observed)

`failover_acquire` uses the winning pod's `CLOCK_REALTIME`-stamped `acquireTime`; `failover_observed` uses only this host's clock (`t_detect − t_kill`). `CLOCK_REALTIME` is not virtualized by time namespaces and kind shares the host kernel, so the two should agree to within one poll interval. Mean |acquire − observed| per config:

| default | 0.090s | 20 |
| stock | 0.086s | 20 |

Sub-decisecond agreement confirms the cross-process timestamp subtraction is valid — there is no hidden clock-domain offset inflating or deflating the numbers.

## 5. Flap onset under control-plane latency (the cost of tight timing)

Egress `netem` delay injected on the **live** source leader's renew path (pod never killed). A leadership change here is a pure renew-deadline flap: the leader could not renew within `RenewDeadline` and stepped down. `FLAP` = `leaseTransitions` incremented within the 20s window.

| config | renewDeadline | 1500ms | 2500ms | 4500ms | 8000ms | 12000ms |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| tight | 2s | stable | FLAP | FLAP | FLAP | FLAP |
| default | 4s | stable | stable | FLAP | FLAP | FLAP |
| stock | 10s | stable | stable | stable | stable | stable |

Flap onset tracks `RenewDeadline`: a leader tolerates a renew round-trip up to ~`RenewDeadline` and demotes beyond it — tight flaps just past 2s, default just past 4s. Tighter timing ⇒ flaps under smaller control-plane latency, the concrete cost of a shorter lease.

> **Stock caveat.** Stock reads `stable` even at 12000 ms (>10s `RenewDeadline`). This is an observation-window artifact, not extra safety: the stock leader still *demotes* at ~`RenewDeadline`, but the standby can only take the Lease after the 15s `LeaseDuration` expires, so the `leaseTransitions` bump lands at/after the 20s probe window. The safety-relevant comparison — tight (2s) vs default (4s) — is unaffected and clean.

## 7. Why `6s / 4s / 1s` is the reasonable default

- **Failover, measured (p50 / max):** tight 3.6/5.1s · default 6.9/7.8s · stock 16.7/19.5s. The default recovers ~2.4× faster than client-go stock.

- **Invariants hold:** `RenewDeadline(4) < LeaseDuration(6)` and `RetryPeriod(1) < RenewDeadline(4)`; with `4s/1s` a leader gets ~4 renew attempts before self-demoting, and `RetryPeriod·1.2 = 1.2s < 4s` keeps renews from starving.

- **Safety margin:** `LeaseDuration − RenewDeadline = 2s` of slack for clock skew / GC pauses / API latency. §5 shows the default tolerates renew RTTs up to ~4s before flapping, vs ~2s for the tight config — the tight config buys only a few seconds of failover for half the latency headroom, a poor trade on a busy control plane.

- **Warm standbys:** failover here is a Lease acquire + a single pipeline POST (connect is already running), so single-digit-second failover is not a bottleneck; stock's larger lease just lengthens the dead-air window after a crash for no correctness gain (§6 all green regardless).


**Conclusion.** `6s/4s/1s` sits at the knee: failover dominated by — and clustering just under — the `LeaseDuration` term (p50 6.9s, p95 7.6s), a 2s safety margin that survives realistic control-plane latency without flapping (§5), and zero correctness cost (§6). Tighter (3s/2s/1s) trades half the latency headroom for ~3s of failover; stock (15s/10s/2s) trades ~10s of failover speed for margin this warm-standby lab does not need.

## Scope & caveats

- Single-node kind on one host: no real network partition, no contended control plane. Flap onset (§5) is induced with synthetic `netem` latency, which models API-path delay but not apiserver CPU starvation.

- `acquireTime`/`renewTime` are client-stamped (winning pod) `CLOCK_REALTIME`; §3 corroborates against a pure single-host-clock metric.

- No-LWW lab: within one sink batch, same-key ops may reorder; the verifier is order-insensitive, so this does not affect the correctness verdict.
